Lecture Content

Image enhancement manipulates images to make them more suitable for a specific application:

  • Highlighting interesting details.
  • Removing noise.
  • Making images visually appealing.
  • Techniques are problem-oriented.

Most spatial domain operations can be reduced to:

g(x,y) = T[f(x,y)]

Where f(x,y) is input image, g(x,y) is processed image, and T is an operator over a neighborhood of (x,y).

Simplest spatial domain operation when neighborhood is just the pixel itself:

s = T(r)

s = processed pixel, r = original pixel

  • Linear: negative, identity transformation.
  • Logarithmic: log and inverse-log.
  • Power-law (gamma): s = c * r^γ
  • Contrast stretching: Thresholding functions.
  • Contrast stretching: increases dynamic range.
  • Intensity-level slicing: highlight specific ranges.
  • Bit-plane slicing: isolates bits to highlight details.

Histogram: graph of gray level occurrence in the image.

  • Histogram equalization: adjusts intensities to enhance contrast.
  • Histogram matching (specification): generate output image with specified histogram.

Spatial filtering applies operations over a neighborhood of pixels:

  • Neighborhood: small rectangle over the image.
  • Predefined operation produces new pixel at center.
  • Types: Linear & Nonlinear filtering.
  • Handling borders: ignore edges or pad with zeros.

Correlation: move filter mask over image, sum of products at each location.

Convolution: same as correlation but mask is rotated 180°.

  • Used for blurring and noise reduction.
  • Low-pass filters: smooth image, reduce noise, obscure fine details.
  • Linear smoothing: averaging (box, weighted).
  • Nonlinear smoothing: order-statistic filters like median, min, max.
  • Median filter effective for salt-and-pepper noise.
